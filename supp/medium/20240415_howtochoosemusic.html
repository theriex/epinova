How to choose music?

Uniqueness, depth of expression, and how much you connect. Simple, but not automatically easy, and after seeing several of my fellow listeners getting a bit lost and overwhelmed, this seems worth writing down in the interest of increased enjoyment.

Terms

Uniqueness is like the opposite of sameness. Sameness is boring. Too much sameness might make you boring. Uniqueness deserves your attention.

Depth of expression reflects the time, energy, and capability of the artist. Simple music can have depth, and complicated music can be shallow. At a base level this is about how much heart and soul you can hear went into the creation of what you are listening to.

How much you connect with it. Note that phrasing. All songs try to connect with you, so it's your choice if you want to let that happen. Choosing music is more about you going to it than it coming to you. If you find a song interesting, compelling, outstanding, or any other superlative, consider revisiting it. If you're revisiting a song, consider collecting it.

Here's how this works in a few music discovery situations.

Matchmaking

When you have excellent music in common with another person, unknown major milestones from their musical journey are probably worth listening to. They found unique qualities and depth, now you might connect also.

As you listen, you will quickly hear if it something sounds like a lot of other things you have. If it's not a lot of other things, perhaps you will differentiate more later. If the song is surprisingly different, give yourself a chance to discover what unique aspects might pull for you.

If you respect your guide's listening capabilities, then the song probably has adequate depth of expression. If you're not hearing much, take a moment to try and discern what they might have found, or ask them what they think is particularly great about it. Most people are happy to describe their favorite music, and it can be fun to learn, even if what you learn ends up more about them than the music.

Sometimes you will connect to a song quickly. If not, listen through anyway and decide at the end if it might be worth a second listen later. If not, at least you've explored more of your musical universe.

Matchmakers can be friends, expert reviewers, professional DJs or other curators whose tastes you trust.

Touring

Listening is musical tourism. Anytime you are online, you have thousands of potential tours to choose from, paid or ad-supported. Some tours can be amazing. Most tours are pretty good, but they tend towards sameness. If you're feeling adventurous, you might have to nudge the operator a bit to avoid getting pigeonholed. One way is to start from a suggested song, or maybe an artist or producer. Genres and labels can be useful but sometimes limiting. How best to nudge the operator depends on the tour, and you may have to experiment a bit.

Finding uniqueness while touring might be rare, but it's rewarding.

Depth of expression is not a primary consideration for most tours. Most artists put quite a bit of time and effort into creating what you are hearing, but how you listen and how they convey might not match well. One advantage of being on a tour is that listening expectations don't vary too much from one song to the next, so trust what you feel when you notice depth.

If you don't discern any uniqueness or depth, and you don't feel an immediate connection, hit the skip button. If you hear anything notable, listen through and decide if it's worth revisiting.

News

Older releases are the structure, context and history of the music world. New releases are the lifeblood. You need both. For the lifeblood part, following a feed of new releases tailored to your current interests makes you an active part of the music scene. Artists want you involved, and what you think matters.

Because there is more music available than anyone can possibly listen to in one lifetime, uniqueness is relative only to your own experience. If you feel it's unique, it's unique.

With new releases, feeling depth of expression can be particularly moving since you and the artist are still close in time. If they put their heart and soul into a song and you feel it, they've succeeded. You might even be able to let them know.

The strange thing about connecting to new releases is that it's not strange. It works exactly the same as any other listening situation except there's no peer pressure. That can be liberating.

Life

Random social posts, broadcast radio, movies, documentaries, live shows, opening music, parties and everything else all work the same way. Uniqueness, depth of expression, and how much you connect with it. Enjoy your music world.
